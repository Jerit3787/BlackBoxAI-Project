Chain Rule

The chain rule is a fundamental theorem in calculus that allows us to compute the derivative of a composite function. If we have two functions, \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\), the chain rule states that:

\[\frac{d}{dx}[f(g(x))] = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)\]

In simpler terms, to differentiate a composite function, we differentiate the outer function and multiply it by the derivative of the inner function.

For example, if \(f(x) = \sin(x)\) and \(g(x) = x^2\), then the derivative of the composite function \(f(g(x)) = \sin(x^2)\) is:

\[\frac{d}{dx}[\sin(x^2)] = \cos(x^2) \cdot 2x\]

Here, we first differentiate the outer function \(\sin(x)\) to get \(\cos(x^2)\), and then multiply it by the derivative of the inner function \(g(x) = x^2\), which is \(2x\).

In practice, the chain rule is used extensively in calculus to differentiate complex functions that are composed of simpler functions.
